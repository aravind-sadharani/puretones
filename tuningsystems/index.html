Indian Tuning Systems

Sama Gana Scale

Pancham and Madhyam bhav are recognisable intervals. They are easy to recognise by ear. It is also easy to determine their frequency ratios in relation to the fundamental, by comparing lengths. So, let us start by pointing out that with Sa = 1 and SA = 2, Pa = 3/2 and ma = 4/3.

Knowing these two, it is possible to derive the ma-Pa interval. This can be learned by people to recognise by ear. The size of the interval can be calculated as 3/2 divided by 4/3 = 9/8.

Using this, the following Sama Gana scale can be derived. The method of derivation is given in the remarks column.

In this scale, the intervals Re-ga and Dha-ni were regarded as Vivadi. The size of these intervals can be calculated to be 256/243 = 1.053497942. This scale may have been appropriate for chanting, but as a musical scale, these gaps needed to be "fixed".

Circle of Fifths

Using the Pancham and Madhyam bhavs, we can also form two scales called the circle of fifths and the circle of fourths. In the circle of fifths, starting from the note Sa, you derive the next note in the scale by taking the fifth of the previously derived note. So, Pa follows from Sa. Then Re follows from Pa. And so on. Here they are (see below). Indian music scholars of old knew these two scales (at least, conceptually and mathematically, if not being able to make a harp practically)

Anyone who is familiar with the circle of fifths and fourths would notice that the after the 12th note is derived (respectively, the ma and the Pa), if you take a fifth or fourth for a 13th time, you would get a ratio which is very close to Sa = 1, but not exactly 1. This gap by which the 13th note, exceeds or falls short respectively, in the circles of fifths and fourths, is called the Pythagorean comma. Its value can be calculated as 531441/524288 = 1.013643265. The ancient Indian music scholars were aware of this phenomenon and aware of the value of the Pythagorean comma

Shadaj Gram Scale

Knowing the Pythagorean comma, it is possible to "fix" the Sama Gana scale and derive the first real scale of Indian Classical music, the Shadaj Gram scale. This can be done by tuning the Re and Dha down in pitch by an amount equal to the Pythagorean comma. Here is the scale (see below).

SA22Octave

If this Shadaj Gram scale is accepted, then one can attach meaningful interpretations to 1 shruti, 2 shruti, 3 shruti and 4 shruti intervals. ma-Pa interval is defined as a 4 shruti interval. Sa-Re interval is defined as a 3 shruti interval. Re-ga interval is defined as a 2 shruti interval. The Re-ga gap in the Sama Gana scale is defined as a 1 shruti interval (1.053497942), while the Pythagorean Comma is also called a 1 shruti interval (1.013643265). Clearly these two 1 shruti intervals are different despite their classification, and so they had different names.
